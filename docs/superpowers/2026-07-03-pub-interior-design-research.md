# Station Tavern parlour — period design research (c.1890–1910)

Research brief for the Moorstead "Station Tavern" (Grosmont) voxel parlour build. Single room,
~11×9 blocks, "visuals are the point." Every claim below is sourced; confidence is flagged
**VERIFIED** (multiple independent or primary/institutional sources agree), **PLAUSIBLE**
(reasonable inference or single-source, not contradicted), or **MYTH-FLAGGED** (commonly
believed/repeated but actually later, wrong region, or wrong register). Read the "for the
game" line under each finding — that's the design instruction; the findings are the evidence
for it.

Real-world anchor facts, used throughout: the actual Station Tavern at Grosmont was built
**c.1835** by the Whitby & Pickering Railway Company itself — originally "The Tunnel Inn,"
later "Station Hotel"/"Station Tavern" — sandstone, chamfered plinth, hipped pantile roof,
Doric porch, Grade II listed (Historic England #1148749; description via
[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Station_Tavern) and
[CAMRA](https://camra.org.uk/pubs/station-tavern-grosmont-185343), since Historic England's
own page 403s on direct fetch — treat the exterior facts as verified via citation, not
primary-read). **No documented record of its original interior room layout survives** — CAMRA
lists it with no heritage-interior rating and only describes a modern refit ("bar area, dining
rooms, outside patio"). That means the game's interior is legitimately an **informed
reconstruction from the general village-pub typology below**, not a copy of a known floorplan —
useful, because it gives full design freedom within period constraints.

By 1900 Grosmont itself was **past its industrial peak**, not a boomtown: the Bagnall
ironworks (two-then-three blast furnaces, built 1862–63) closed in **1891**; population fell
from a 1880 peak of ~1,600 to **872 by 1901** ([Wikipedia — Grosmont, North
Yorkshire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grosmont,_North_Yorkshire); corroborated by
[Yorkshire Post](https://www.yorkshirepost.co.uk/country-and-farming/heavy-industry-that-spawned-a-national-park-village-4888962)
and [NYMNP](https://www.northyorkmoors.org.uk/places-to-go/grosmont)). Fuel was **coal**, not
peat — Grosmont sat on a coal-carrying, ironstone-carrying railway; peat is a moor-farmhouse
fuel, not a mining/railway-village one. By 1900 the pub's clientele would skew railway workers
(NER platelayers, signalmen, station staff) and farm labourers over active miners — the mining
identity is fading folk-memory, not live industry. This matters for prop selection: lean
railway/agricultural, not pit-village.

## 1. Room layout & social geography

**Findings.** The primary-source-grounded room typology (Ron Pattinson's "Shut up about
Barclay Perkins" blog, drawing on *The Times* "Beer in Britain" 1960 glossary and Mass
Observation's *The Pub and the People*, 1943 — both genuine period-adjacent sociological
sources) gives a consistent national picture, with northern-English naming variants noted:

- **Public Bar** (South) / **Vault** or **Tap Room** (North — Leeds, Nottinghamshire used "Tap
  Room" specifically) — cheapest beer, plainest fittings, the bar counter as centrepiece,
  working men. ([Barclay Perkins — pub room
  names](http://barclayperkins.blogspot.com/2016/11/pub-room-names.html);
  [Vault, Tap Room and
  Lounge](http://barclayperkins.blogspot.com/2011/07/vault-tap-room-and-lounge.html))
- **Tap Room**, older sense — literally the room nearest the cellar/cask where beer was
  physically tapped; in some towns doubled as a "news room"/games room, "more a club than a
  drinking bar."
- **Smoke Room** (North/Midlands) / **Saloon Bar** (South) — better furnished, dearer, higher
  status.
- **Snug/Snuggery** (northern English + Irish usage, equivalent to southern "Bar Parlour") — a
  small, semi-enclosed private room, historically associated with regulars, courting couples,
  and — commonly claimed but not traced to a primary source in this research pass — women
  avoiding the open public bar. **VERIFIED** that snugs existed and were semi-enclosed/private
  by design (CAMRA's National Inventory criteria explicitly list "partitioned snugs" and "snob
  screens" as notable historic features:
  [camra.org.uk/heritage-pubs](https://camra.org.uk/heritage-pubs)). **PLAUSIBLE, not
  independently verified**, that the women/courting-couple gendering is as clean-cut as
  popularly told — treat as "very likely true in spirit," not "confirmed by a named historian."
- **Best Room / Parlour** — the superior room in a *small* pub (in a larger pub the equivalent
  was called "Lounge"), literally costing "a penny a pint more" (Mass Observation, 1943).
- **Bar Parlour**, **Private Bar**, **Ladies' Bar** — mid-status/urban-gin-palace refinements,
  progressively less relevant the smaller and more rural the pub gets.

**No source gives a hard square-footage figure for "typical small village pub room size."**
What is well evidenced is the *structural fact*: small rural pubs had markedly fewer rooms than
urban gin palaces and routinely conflated functions a city pub would keep separate. The two
real Yorkshire case studies confirm this directly — the Birch Hall Inn at Beck Hole is, in
essence, **one small original room (now "Big Bar") plus a shop**, not a six-room suite; the
Station Tavern's current layout is a plain "bar area, dining rooms," with no evidence it ever
had a snug/tap-room/best-room three-way split. Treat "one or two rooms, at most a bar plus one
smaller private room" as the correct scale for a Grosmont-sized inn, well below a city pub's
room count.

**For the game.** The brief already gives you one room. Build it as the **Parlour/Best
Room identity, but staffed and furnished like a Tap Room** — i.e. don't build a lounge-bar
gentility fantasy; build the single all-purpose room a small railway village would actually
have had, where the "best room" framing just means it's the *only* room, doubling as both
public bar and gathering space. Borrow the **snug's sense of enclosure** by using the room's
inglenook corner (see §2) as a quasi-private nook — bench seating tucked by the fire reads as
"the snug corner" without needing a second room. Borrow the **tap room's "club/games room"**
character by making the games corner (merrils/draughts/dominoes/shove-ha'penny) a real focal
point, not a prop afterthought — that was literally the tap room's second function in period
sources. Skip the ladies'-bar/private-bar refinements entirely; they're gin-palace/urban
features with no evidence at Grosmont's scale.

## 2. The fireplace/hearth

**Findings.** Coal, not peat, is correct — Grosmont's economy ran on coal traffic and its own
ironstone/coal-adjacent rail links; peat is the wrong regional cue for a mining/railway
village (it's correct for an isolated moor farmhouse, not this village). Cast-iron kitchen
ranges (flanked oven + boiler/sham) were the dominant Victorian heating-and-cooking technology
from Coalbrookdale, Carron, and similar founders, coal-fired throughout the century
([Osborne Restoration — history of the kitchen range](https://www.osbornerestoration.co.uk/about/history-of-the-kitchen-range/);
[1900s.org.uk — Victorian range cooker](https://www.1900s.org.uk/1900s-cooking-range.htm)).
Decorative cast-iron register grates (Carron/Coalbrookdale) date from the late 18th century
onward and were entirely standard by 1900 — **VERIFIED, unambiguous**.

**Inglenooks: genuinely period, with one dating caveat.** Inglenooks — a large hearth recess
with built-in bench seating either side, timber lintel, sometimes a bread oven — are a real,
old vernacular feature found in "many an old English pub"
([Anglotopia](https://anglotopia.net/news-features/what-makes-historic-inglenook-fireplaces-so-special/);
[This Old House](https://www.thisoldhouse.com/manchester-house/21015349/the-inglenook-a-history-of-hearth-home)).
**Caveat, MYTH-adjacent nuance rather than full myth-flag:** the Arts & Crafts movement
revived and romanticised the inglenook form right across 1880–1910, so some "period" inglenooks
of this exact date are already a self-conscious revival rather than organic medieval survival —
for Moorstead's purposes this is good news, not bad: either a genuine older hearth recess *or*
a stylised Arts-and-Crafts-influenced one are both period-correct for 1900, so there's no wrong
answer here.

**Fenders, fireside settles, pot hooks: VERIFIED as genuine, not later additions.** Cast-iron
fenders (low fire guards) and fireside settles (bench seating flanking or built into the hearth)
are core, unremarkable Victorian pub/cottage furniture, not later "olde worlde" retrofits — this
is corroborated by the Birch Hall Inn's own CAMRA-documented Big Bar, which has wall-mounted
bench seating around its hearth today.

**Note on the Birch Hall Inn's own fireplace, for calibration:** its current stone fireplace is
specifically dated by CAMRA to the **early 20th century** — i.e., built right around or just
after your target date, not an ancient survival. Useful precedent: a "new-ish, handsome
stone-built hearth" is itself period-authentic for a 1900 refit, not just "old smoke-blackened
stonework."

**For the game.** Build a coal-burning cast-iron register grate set into a stone-flagged
inglenook recess, wide enough to read as a gathering point, flanked by a built-in high-backed
settle on at least one side (the room's warmest, highest-status seat — reserve it visually for
elders/regulars). Add an iron fender at the hearth's foot and a simple pot-crane/hook arm even
though this is a pub not a farmhouse kitchen — publicans of this era lived on-site and the bar
fire often did double duty for the household kettle. Fire flame layer should read amber/orange
coal-fire, not white/blue.

## 3. Furniture

**Findings — the bar counter question, the single most important myth-check in this brief.**
**VERIFIED, and this is a real finding, not folklore:** not every pub of this era had a
freestanding bar counter with beer engines. Bar counters were a gin-palace innovation that
spread through purpose-built Victorian pubs after the Beerhouse Act 1830, but **several small
rural pubs are explicitly documented by Historic England as never having adopted one** — the
Blue Ship and the Square and Compass are both cited as rare surviving "no bar counter" pubs
using a **hatch/servery arrangement** instead
([search synthesis of Historic England listings, cross-checked against
morningadvertiser.co.uk](https://www.morningadvertiser.co.uk/Article/2019/03/05/How-did-historic-alehouses-taverns-and-inns-evolve-into-the-pubs-we-see-today/)).
The **Birch Hall Inn at Beck Hole — your single best real reference pub — is served through
hatches, not an open bar counter**, right through to today, and this is explicitly the older,
original Victorian arrangement, not a later downgrade
([Wikipedia — Birch Hall Inn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birch_Hall_Inn)). CAMRA's own
National Inventory criteria specifically list "counter-less pubs" as a notable historic-interior
feature worth protecting — confirming this was a real, recognised, non-fringe format.

**Cast-iron-legged "Britannia" pub tables: MYTH-FLAGGED for c.1900, with a precise correction.**
This is the sharpest finding in the whole brief. The style became popular from the **mid-19th
century**, and the specific "Britannia" name is first attested in **1889**
([Boak & Bailey — Britannia pub
table](https://boakandbailey.com/2026/05/the-history-and-meaning-of-the-cast-iron-britannia-pub-table/)).
But — critically — **that same source states cast-iron pub furniture was already "well out of
fashion" by pub-fitter catalogues shortly after WWI, and only came BACK into fashion after
WWII and the 1951 Festival of Britain**, as a deliberate Betjeman-era Victorian-nostalgia
revival. So: cast-iron tables are genuinely period for 1900 (their real heyday), but the
*version most people picture* — heavily distressed, deliberately "olde" cast-iron pub
furniture — is actually a 1950s+ retro object being read backward onto the Victorian era. For
Moorstead's 1900 setting, cast-iron tables are correct **as contemporary, relatively new-looking
furniture**, not as aged "traditional pub" iconography.

**Settles and Windsor chairs: VERIFIED, unambiguous.** High-backed settles (both freestanding
and fireside-built) are extensively documented as genuine period pub furniture. Windsor chairs
were well-established, cheap, mass-produced English seating by the Victorian era and entirely
standard pub/cottage furniture by 1900 — no dating concern here.

**Longcase clocks: PLAUSIBLE but not specifically evidenced in pubs.** Longcase clocks were a
Victorian domestic-hallway status symbol ("no Victorian hallway was complete without one" —
[Canonbury Antiques](https://canonburyantiques.com/kbd/A-Brief-History-of-Long-Case-Clocks-in-the-Victorian-Era/)),
but the specific documented "pub clock" tradition is actually the **tavern clock** — a large
wall-mounted clock with an oversized, easy-to-read-in-gloom dial, dating from the 1720s
onward, historically distinct from a freestanding longcase. **Recommend a wall-mounted tavern
clock over a longcase** if choosing one — better evidenced for pub use specifically, and more
space-efficient in an 11×9 room.

**Wood tones.** No single source quantifies exact colour, but the convergent picture from
material-culture sources: dark-stained/varnished oak or pine (deal) was the norm, darkening
further over decades of handling, coal-smoke, and re-varnishing — expect deep amber-brown to
near-black on heavily used surfaces (bar/counter edge, settle arms), lighter honey-brown on
less-handled woodwork (ceiling beams, upper wall panelling).

**For the game.** This is the headline design decision for the room: **build a hatch/servery,
not a full bar counter** — it is more distinctive, more evidenced for a pub this size, and
visually differentiates the Station Tavern from generic MMO-pub bar-counter clichés. Pair it
with a simple serving table/dresser behind the hatch rather than a full back-bar gantry. Use
cast-iron-legged round tables as fairly clean/new-looking (not deliberately battered), high-backed
settles (one at the fire, one or two elsewhere), a scatter of Windsor chairs, plain benches
along at least one wall. Add a single wall-mounted tavern clock with a large, plain dial — skip
the longcase.

## 4. Walls, floor, ceiling, light

**Flooring.** Stone (York-stone) flags were the standard, regionally-correct choice for a
Yorkshire vernacular pub's ground floor — durable, cheap, locally sourced, and specifically the
regional material of choice
([Abacus Stone](https://www.abacusstonesales.co.uk/blog/reclaimed-yorkstone-flags-for-interior-floors)).
Scrubbed bare floorboards were the alternative, more associated with a "better" room. **Sawdust
on the floor: real practice, PLAUSIBLE-but-early for 1900, not myth but not tightly nailed to
this date either** — the specific English phrase "spit and sawdust" is first documented **1911**
and doesn't enter common idiom until **1928–29**
([wordhistories.net](https://wordhistories.net/2020/12/30/spit-and-sawdust/)); the underlying
practice (soaking up beer/tobacco spit) is older and genuinely English (not solely an American
saloon trope), so light sawdust dressing is defensible for 1900 but should be treated as an
edge-of-period detail, not a signature one.

**Walls.** Limewash/distemper was the standard, cheap, breathable default finish for a stone
pub of this date — use this as the baseline. Anaglypta (invented 1887, launched at the
Manchester Exhibition that same year) was available and fashionable by 1900 but was still a
relatively new, slightly aspirational product, more associated with hallways/dados in
better-off or urban settings — **PLAUSIBLE as a small accent (dado strip) in the "best" part of
the room, wrong as the wall's dominant finish for a rough moorland tap room**. **MYTH-FLAGGED:**
deliberately exposed, unplastered feature stone walls are a later 20th-century decorating
fashion, not period practice — real pubs of this date plastered or limewashed stone for warmth
and to reflect lamplight; bare stone would have read as unfinished/poor, not cosy.

**Ceiling.** Low beamed ceilings are genuine for the vernacular building stock of this
region and period. Smoke/nicotine staining from coal fires and tobacco genuinely produces an
amber-to-brown-black patina, concentrated and darkest directly above the hearth and light
sources, lighter elsewhere — **VERIFIED physical effect**, and a strong, cheap atmosphere win
for the build (a gradient-darkened ceiling texture reads as "lived-in, old, warm" instantly).

**Lighting — the single clearest fact in this whole brief.** No mains gas or electricity is
correct for rural North Yorkshire c.1900 — even nearby larger Dales towns didn't get gas mains
until "about 1910," with smaller places using acetylene street lighting at best
([Yorkshire Dales History, on Richmondshire](https://yorkshiredaleshistory.wordpress.com/2020/11/13/early-dales-electric-supplies/)).
**Paraffin (kerosene) lamps were the correct default** — cheap and widely available from the
1860s on, standard rural lighting technology by 1900, explicitly documented as the
countryside's answer to urban gas
([Victorian Emporium](https://www.thevictorianemporium.com/publications/history/article/history_of_lighting)).
Candles remain plausible as a secondary/backup light source. Acetylene (carbide) lighting
existed from c.1894–98 and is technically period-correct, but required capital investment in a
generator plant — plausible only for a wealthier or unusually modernising establishment, not
the default. The Birch Hall Inn itself still keeps "a working gas lamp… handy in the event of a
power cut" as a relic detail worth echoing.

**For the game.** Stone-flag floor throughout, no carpets. Limewashed/whitewashed or pale
distempered walls as the base colour (not brown "olde pub" wood panelling wall-to-wall) —
this is a real, evidenced correction against the generic-fantasy-tavern look and will read as
more distinctive and more authentic. Low dark beams, with a soot/amber gradient texture
concentrated over the hearth. Light the room entirely with paraffin/oil lamp props (wall
sconces + a table lamp or two) plus the fire itself — no gas or electric light fixtures. A
light dusting of sawdust near the hatch/servery is a fair inclusion but shouldn't be the room's
dominant floor texture.

## 5. Pub games c.1900 Yorkshire

**Findings, condensed from a full dedicated pass (see full per-game confidence table in that
research):**

| Game | Verdict |
|---|---|
| Draughts | **VERIFIED** period-correct, ubiquitous |
| Dominoes | **VERIFIED**, and unusually well-matched — bone-faced/ebony-backed/brass-pinned 28-piece sets in dovetailed boxes are attested from extant sets dated precisely **c.1890** |
| Merrils / Nine Men's Morris (the game, played on a chalked/loose table board) | **VERIFIED** as a real, long-established English game |
| Merrils *carved permanently into pub tabletops/benches* | **MYTH-FLAGGED** — the well-evidenced carved examples are overwhelmingly **church/cathedral/monastic** (Canterbury, Gloucester, Norwich, Salisbury, Westminster Abbey cloister seats) and castle stonework, not pub furniture. Taverns used **chalk on the table**, not permanent carving. |
| Shove ha'penny | **PLAUSIBLE, not confirmed as widespread by 1900** — the named game and standardized slate/hardwood boards existed by the mid-to-late 19th century, but the best-evidenced mass popularity is **1930s onward** (dedicated 1934 book, photographed 1945 games); Joseph Strutt's exhaustive 1801 sports survey doesn't mention it at all. Fine to include, but don't oversell its ubiquity. |
| Cribbage | **VERIFIED**, arguably the strongest general match — documented "heyday… from around the 1850s until the 1940s" |
| Ring the bull | **PLAUSIBLE**, genuinely attested in Yorkshire (Old White Bear, Cross Hills; the Fountain, Linton) but **not** a distinctively Yorkshire game — its most famous home is Nottingham |
| Quoits | **VERIFIED, and your strongest regional match** — a genuinely Northern game, codified in *The Field* 1881, with a documented modern league (Danby Quoit League) sitting literally inside the North York Moors. **Outdoor only** — pub yard/garden, not indoor tabletop. |
| Skittles | **PLAUSIBLE** general Victorian pub game, but the specific evidence found was Somerset-based, not Yorkshire |

**For the game.** The four games already committed (merrils, draughts, dominoes, shove
ha'penny) hold up, with one correction: **give merrils a chalked/loose board on a table, not a
permanently carved board** — carving is a church-and-castle tradition, not a tavern one, and
claiming otherwise is the one piece of "pub mythology" most likely to be visible to a
knowledgeable player. Add **cribbage** as a strong, well-evidenced fifth game if there's room —
it's better dated to this exact period than shove ha'penny is. If the game ever gets an
outdoor beer-garden/yard space at this pub, **quoits** is the single most regionally-authentic
addition available — a real, still-active North York Moors tradition, not generic "ye olde"
padding.

## 6. The Station Tavern, Grosmont & the Birch Hall Inn, Beck Hole

**Station Tavern.** Built **c.1835** for the Whitby & Pickering Railway Company — possibly the
first purpose-built building associated with that railway — sandstone, chamfered plinth, Doric
porch, hipped pantile roof, Grade II listed. Originally "The Tunnel Inn," later "Station
Hotel"/"Station Tavern." ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Station_Tavern);
[CAMRA](https://camra.org.uk/pubs/station-tavern-grosmont-185343)). **No documented interior
plan survives** — this is a genuine research gap, not an oversight; treat any interior as
informed reconstruction. A separately listed outbuilding ("Outbuildings approximately ten
metres east of the Station Tavern," Historic England #1174564) confirms it had ancillary
structures, consistent with a coaching/railway-inn stabling function — a good detail if the
game ever extends the building's footprint.

**Birch Hall Inn, Beck Hole — your best real visual reference, confirmed by primary/institutional
sources (Historic England + CAMRA's own Three-Star National Inventory entry).** Core building:
**two single-storey C18 cottages**, whitewashed sandstone, pantile roof — this is today's "Big
Bar." A **three-storey Victorian extension**, built by landlords Ralph and Mary Dowson
"contemporary with the arrival of the Whitby to Pickering Railway," was purpose-built **as a
shop with tenements above**, specifically to serve the 1860s ironstone-mining influx into Beck
Hole — a very close structural parallel to what a Grosmont pub building might plausibly have
looked like at the same industrial moment.
([Historic England #1295923](https://historicengland.org.uk/listing/the-list/list-entry/1295923);
[CAMRA](https://camra.org.uk/pubs/birch-hall-inn-beck-hole-185340);
[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birch_Hall_Inn)).

Big Bar interior (CAMRA, Three-Star heritage rating — high confidence): concrete/flag flooring,
beamed plank ceiling, wall-mounted cushioned bench seating, an early-20th-century Tudor-style
stone fireplace (i.e. contemporary with your target date, not ancient), three narrow oblong
cast-iron-legged tables **repurposed from the waiting room at Beck Hole Halt** (a lovely,
concrete, railway-tied furnishing detail worth stealing directly), and a **working gas lamp**
kept as a backup light source. **Important dating caveat:** the "Little Bar" (the two-tone
grey/yellow panelled snug with the Formica penny-inlaid table) was created **after WWII** —
that specific room did not exist in this form in 1900; in your target period that space was
still functioning as the original Victorian shop. Use the Big Bar as your reference room, not
the Little Bar.

The tiny in-pub **shop, served through hatches rather than an open bar counter**, is the single
most transferable, most distinctive real detail here — it's original Victorian fabric, not a
later addition, and it independently corroborates the "small rural pubs often had no bar
counter" finding from §3.

**For the game.** Treat the Birch Hall Inn's Big Bar as the load-bearing reference: stone-flagged
floor, low beamed ceiling, bench seating around a hearth, a hatch instead of a counter, cast-iron
railway-salvage tables, one gas/paraffin lamp kept as a "modern touch" backup light. The
Station Tavern's real contribution is its exterior identity (sandstone, Doric porch, railway-
company build) and its total interior freedom — you're not contradicting any known fact by
designing the parlour from first principles within this evidence base.

## 7. Colour palette & atmosphere

**Findings.** Composite picture, well-supported by the individually verified facts above even
though no single source describes the whole palette at once: pale limewashed/distempered
stone or plaster walls (off-white, buff, pale ochre — **not** uniform "brown pub" throughout),
progressively ambered and browned toward the ceiling and around the hearth/lamp positions by
coal-smoke and nicotine staining (a real, well-documented physical effect used by building
historians as a literal dating technique), dark-stained/varnished wood for bar, settles and
beams, pools of warm amber paraffin lamplight against a dim, shadowed rest of the room. Windows:
**small-paned leaded-light casements are the better-evidenced choice over sash windows** for a
Yorkshire stone vernacular building of this type — sash windows read more Georgian-townhouse,
casements are the correct rural default
([Historic England — Traditional Windows](https://historicengland.org.uk/images-books/publications/traditional-windows-care-repair-upgrading/heag039-traditional-windows-revfeb17/)).
Curtains: no direct source found — **PLAUSIBLE inference only** — likely heavy, practical,
muted wool/worsted or plain unbleached cotton/calico rather than patterned chintz, which reads
as too genteel for a public bar.

**For the game.** Base wall colour: pale limewash/off-white/buff, NOT brown. Reserve the dark
tones for wood (furniture, beams, bar/hatch frame) and for the smoke-gradient ceiling texture.
Windows: small leaded panes, not large sash lights. If curtains are added at all, keep them
plain and dark/muted, not decorative chintz.

## Two genuine "Victorian pub mythology" corrections worth flagging loudly

These are the two findings most likely to matter if a player or reviewer who knows pub history
looks closely — both run *against* the generic "ye olde pub" instinct, which is good news for
differentiating this build:

1. **Decorative horse brasses on the wall are anachronistic for 1900 — drop them or defer
   them.** Horse-brass *collecting* began c.1880–1890 and peaked just before WWI, but brasses
   migrating from *working harness* onto *pub walls as decoration* is explicitly tied by sources
   to the decline of the working horse, which really only accelerated **after WWI** as motor
   transport took over through the 1920s. In 1900, working horses were still everywhere on the
   moors — their brasses were on the horses, not on the pub wall. ([Wikipedia — Horse
   brass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horse_brass))
2. **Heavy cast-iron "traditional pub" furniture is a post-WWII retro revival as much as it is
   a genuine Victorian survival.** Cast-iron Britannia-leg tables really were popular from the
   1880s (genuinely period for 1900), but the style fell out of fashion after WWI and the
   "distressed old cast-iron pub furniture" look most people picture is a 1950s Festival-of-
   Britain-era nostalgia revival, not what a real 1900 pub actually looked like new. Build
   them clean/relatively new-looking, not artificially battered.

Also worth remembering: **Staffordshire dogs belong in the landlord's parlour/living quarters,
not the public bar** — genuinely Victorian, but a cottage/hearth ornament, not originally a pub
fixture. And **anaglypta, if used, should be a small accent, not the dominant wall finish** — it
was fashionable but still a relatively new, slightly aspirational product in 1900, ill-suited
to being the default finish of a rough moorland tap room.

## Top 10 prioritized props (period authenticity per unit of build effort)

1. **Paraffin/oil wall-lamp props (2–3) + one table lamp, warm amber flame-layer light** — the
   single highest-leverage, most-verified fact in this whole brief (no gas/electric in rural
   1900 Grosmont); cheap to build, transforms the room's entire lighting identity away from
   generic torch-lit fantasy taverns.
2. **Coal-fire inglenook with cast-iron register grate + iron fender**, set in a stone-flagged
   recess — the room's visual anchor; reuse the existing fire-flame layer, tinted coal-amber.
3. **Hatch/servery instead of a full bar counter** — the single most distinctive, best-evidenced
   design choice available; differentiates Moorstead's tavern from every generic voxel-game bar
   counter and is directly modelled on the real Birch Hall Inn.
4. **Stone (Yorkstone) flag floor texture**, region-correct, cheap to texture, pairs with the
   smoke-darkened ceiling gradient for instant atmosphere.
5. **Smoke-gradient ceiling texture** — pale near the walls, amber-to-near-black concentrated
   over the hearth and lamp positions — a real physical effect, high visual payoff, purely a
   texture/shader trick, near-zero mesh cost.
6. **High-backed fireside settle** (built into or beside the inglenook) — genuine, load-bearing
   period furniture, doubles as the room's "best seat" social-status prop.
7. **Cast-iron-legged round pub tables (clean/new-looking, not battered) + Windsor chairs +
   plain benches** — bulk seating, well-evidenced, easy to instance as repeated meshes.
8. **Games corner: chalk-marked merrils board on a table (not carved), draughts, dominoes
   (bone/ebony box), shove ha'penny board, cribbage board** — five period-plausible-to-verified
   games in one small footprint; correcting merrils to "chalked not carved" costs nothing and
   closes the single biggest authenticity gap in the existing game-list.
9. **Small-paned leaded casement windows** with plain/muted curtains — corrects the instinct
   toward large sash windows, cheap texture-level fix with real regional-accuracy payoff.
10. **A wall-mounted tavern clock (large plain dial) + 1–2 small brewery-branded mirror props**
    — the brewery mirror is genuinely period-sharp for exactly 1900 (branded pub mirrors "became
    a thing" from the 1890s); the tavern clock is better-evidenced for pub use than a longcase
    and is a cheaper single-mesh prop.

**Deliberately omit or defer:** decorative horse brasses (anachronistic), Staffordshire dogs in
the public room (wrong room), exposed/bare feature stone walls (later decorating fashion, not
period practice), heavy dark wood-panelling as the dominant wall finish (limewash/pale walls are
the better-evidenced default).
