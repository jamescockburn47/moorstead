MOORSTEAD

A voxel sandbox of the North York Moors. How I built it, how it works, and what it taught me.
🛠️ Co-developed & rigorously playtested by Henry (8) & James (12), whose detailed feedback shaped the game.
← Back to the moor

Moorstead is a browser sandbox of the real North York Moors, and I built the whole of it without writing a line of code. I'm a commercial litigator, not a programmer. I made it by directing AI models in plain English, and holding what they wrote to the standard I'd hold a junior.

Two things set it apart. The folk who live here don't just talk, they live: around a hundred period villagers go about AI-driven working lives across the dales and the railway, thinking on language models I run on a small computer in my house, with no cloud in the loop. And a single AI agent is woven right through it, as a character you can meet, as the warden that runs the live game, and as the coder that writes new content into it on request.

You explore, build, mine, farm, herd sheep with a real working dog, trade in old money, sail the coast and swim the becks, gather at the tavern of an evening, and ride a steam train across a moor rebuilt block by block from Ordnance Survey data, through real seasons and their festivals, on a phone or at a desk, alongside other players and folk who remember you. The detail's in the boxes below.

1. What it is

Moorstead is a first-person voxel sandbox, the genre Minecraft made familiar, set on the North York Moors: a high heather plateau cut by named dales, blanket bog on the tops, and the North Sea behind cliffs to the east. The fixed landmarks sit where they really are: Roseberry Topping, the Hole of Horcum, the Wainstones, the moor crosses, the Rosedale ironstone kilns, and the ruined abbey above Whitby. Steam trains run the lines, in the shape of the real railway that crossed the moors around 1900. It runs in any browser, and plays on a phone or tablet with full on-screen touch controls as readily as with a mouse and keyboard at a desk.

Moorstead is now a single world: the real North York Moors built from Ordnance Survey data, the actual relief of the place, the real coastline, the real rivers, with the towns and the railway sited where they truly were around 1900. There was once a second, stylised moor, the original build, grown in code from a single number (a "seed") by noise functions. It has been retired. Everyone who plays now, solo or together, walks the same surveyed land.

There is still not a single image, sound, or 3D model file in the project. Textures are painted into a canvas at load time, every creature is assembled from boxes in a few lines of geometry, and all the sound is synthesised. What the survey changed is where the shape of the land comes from: not from noise any more, but from the measured height of the actual place.

The real moor, all the way back to Ordnance Survey

The original build was a believable invention. This one is the place itself. I took a digital elevation model of the whole map, the Ordnance Survey's own height data for the North York Moors, and turned it into the voxel landscape block by block. The relief you walk is the real relief: the high plateau, the way the dales actually cut down through it, the drop to the coast. The coastline and cliffs are the real coastline, taken from the same data rather than drawn by hand, and the sea sits where the land truly meets it. The rivers are the real rivers, the Esk and the rest, carved down their actual courses from the high moor to the sea, with the Esk valley running west to east the way it does on the map. None of the geography is invented. When something is where it is in Moorstead, it is because that is where it is on the ground.

The real layout, around 1900

The towns sit where they really sat. Whitby at the mouth of the Esk, Pickering to the south, Grosmont, Goathland, Lealholm, Danby and the rest of the dale settlements in their true places along the valleys. The railway runs where it really ran around 1900, as three real lines: the Whitby and Pickering through the heart of the moor, the Esk Valley line down the dale, and the Coast line along the cliffs. Each is routed through its real stations. The aim was that anyone who knows the moors should recognise where they are without being told.

A real railway engine

The track is not a rough approximation. It is a smooth curve (a Catmull-Rom spline) threaded through the real station sites, with a proper gradient profile: where the real line needed a cutting or an embankment, the terrain is carved or built up to carry the track at a sensible grade, and where it crossed a river or the Esk estuary there are bridges and a viaduct. The steam trains run to a fixed timetable, computed from the clock, so you can read the boards, plan to be on a platform, and catch a service the way you would a real one. The same timetable is what the villagers ride, below.

An economy, and a working farm

The moor now has a real economy, kept in old money. The currency is brass, counted in pounds, shillings and pence, the coinage Britain used before decimal, partly so a child playing learns to read it. Prices vary by place: a good is cheap where it is made and dear where it is scarce, so coal is worth little at the pit-head and more at the coast, and sea fish is worth more the further inland it travels. That spread is the whole point of trade. You can sell to a villager on the spot for a poor price, or book a rail shipment to a distant market for the full value and collect the brass when the train gets it there. Each station now carries two plain boards, one for the train departures and one for the goods market.

The tameable animals have grown into hill farming. You can take on a sheepdog and whistle her with the real shepherd's calls, come-bye and away to send her round either side, walk on to press, lie down to settle, and so work a scattered flock into a body. Build a fold from hurdles with a one-way field gate, drive the sheep in, and they settle as your own kept stock. The gate is the small trick that makes a sealed pen usable: it opens to an animal coming in off the moor but never lets one back out.

Once you have five head penned you can register a farm at the Moorstead notice board for a pound, which makes you a registered farmer of the parish. After that you can muster the flock, drove it across the moor to the Moorstead mart with your dog, and sell the live animals there. A live beast is worth far more than its wool and mutton sold piecemeal, so this is the best money on the moor. But a flock that strings out loses stragglers to the moor, and after dark the barghest will take them, so keep them together and travel by day.

Claims, decay, and licensed mining: design for engagement and anti-griefing

Rather than letting the voxel landscape become a lawless, grief-prone wasteland, Moorstead implements rules that encourage cooperative engagement and protect the environment. Open-cast strip mining is forbidden by the parish. You cannot dig more than one block below the original terrain grade unless you are inside a designated public quarry or a licensed mine. Public quarries at Moorstead, Goathland, and Pickering allow free deep digging for everyone, and the rock there regenerates over time to prevent permanent unsightly scarring. To open a mine elsewhere, place a Mine Entrance block and register a Mining Licence at a notice board for a fee.

This licensing and upkeep system drives engagement with the trade economy; players must mine, transport, and sell goods to afford their mine's weekly upkeep. Deeper mines require better equipment and safety fixtures: you must install Pit Props, a Safety Lamp, or a Winch to dig into deeper bands, using gritstone and iron picks. Mining also yields Prospecting XP; rare ores like Whitby Jet and Polyhalite are level-gated, so novices receive only bare stone.

To protect your builds from griefing, you can stake a land claim. Active claims prevent your buildings from decaying; unclaimed builds decay after 30 days. If your claim's weekly upkeep lapses, the builds will crumble gradually over time. In the children's world, claims and builds decay twice as slow. Finally, keeping two or more animals of the same species in an active claim allows them to breed tamed baby offspring, rewarding active animal husbandry.

The water, and the coast

The becks and rivers are not painted-on blue. The water flows: each river carries a real current down its course, faster in the narrows, and it pushes you along if you wade or swim in it, so the Esk runs to the sea and you can feel which way is downhill. Step out of your depth and you swim, treading water, ducking under, coming up for air. To the east the land ends at the real North Sea coast, cut from the same survey, with a moving shoreline where the surf breaks and foams against the cliffs and the sands, and fishing cobles drawn up on the coast. After rain the ground itself goes wet and dark and drips from the eaves, and dries again as the weather clears. None of it is a texture swap; it is the same "everything is a function of the state of the world" idea, pointed at water.

The sky, the seasons, and the festivals

The sky runs off the real clock. The sun and moon cross it, the stars come out at night, and the light and shadow move with the hour, so dusk on the moor is a real dusk to plan around (the barghest walks after dark). The four seasons turn the land over the year: the heather and the trees change, and winter brings snow that settles on the tops and the roofs. Over the top of that runs the live weather, tied to the real conditions at Goathland (below), so when the real moor is under mist, so is the game. And the year has its festivals: carols and a decorated tree and lit windows at Christmas, a bonfire on the fifth of November, a maypole in spring, midsummer, harvest and Easter, each with its own procedurally-played bells and music and its own dressing on the villages. All of it, again, is computed rather than stored: no music file, no snow texture, no painted sky.

The tavern of an evening

Each of the dale villages has its inn, and the inn is where the moor gathers. Push the door and you drop into the cellars beneath it, a warren of low stone rooms far bigger than the building above: a vaulted tap room round a cast-iron range, a games room, a screened snug, a servery, letting rooms with beds, and a sunken strongroom. Of an evening the villagers come in off the moor to drink and sit by the fire, and you can join them. You can sit down at a table and play the old pub games for brass, real working engines for merrils (nine men's morris), draughts, dominoes and shove-ha'penny, against an opponent whose sharpness fits who they are. And the inn is a refuge: warm through out of the cold and the wet, stash your goods in the strongbox so that a death on the moor never costs you them, and if enough drinkers are of a mind you can vote the night away and wake warm and rested rather than frozen out on the tops.

2. How I built it

I made Moorstead by instructing AI models in natural language and reviewing what they produced. There was no team, and I had no prior game-development background.

The first playable build came together in a single concentrated session with Fable 5. That was the terrain generation, the voxel engine, movement, building, and the architectural decisions everything else now rests on. The speed was not incidental, and neither was the model. Section 10 is mostly about why. Fable 5 was banned shortly afterwards. The rest of the work, on a less capable model (Opus 4.8), added the visible features: the railway, the talking villagers, the shared multiplayer moor, seasons and live weather, rideable ponies, sailing, tameable animals, the folklore quest line, and the in-world wizard below. Later work added a brass money economy and a working sheepdog and hill farming, both described above. That this phase took far longer, and that a weaker model could carry it, is the lesson. Once the architecture is sound, extending it is ordinary work. The scarce moment was that first session.

The second build is the larger one, and the one this page leads with. It rebuilt the world from the real Ordnance Survey data (Section 3), routed the real c.1900 railway through its real stations, gave the villagers genuine agency over their own days (Section 7), moved their thinking onto a tiered local AI that can serve a crowd (Section 8), and added a routine that lets the world improve itself overnight (Section 9). All of it was still done the same way: by directing AI in plain English and holding the result to a standard, not by typing the code myself.

I built the whole of it in evenings and weekends. It was not vibe-coding in the loose sense. The whole thing is under version control, and I made every change survive a headless test suite (npm run verify, around forty independent checks) before it shipped. The railway is proven never to cross itself, run through a building, or stand in water across multiple seeds. The train's cab view is proven by casting rays from the driver's seat to confirm you can actually see out of the windows. For the real-moor world, a check confirms the voxel terrain matches the Ordnance Survey heights, that the same landscape comes out identically on the server and in the browser, that the rivers run downhill to the sea, and that the landmarks land at their true grid references. Resource veins, landmarks, seasons, weather, pets, villagers, the brass economy, the sheepdog, and the living roster each have their own check. I encoded the discipline an engineer would bring as automated proofs the AI had to pass.

3. The architecture, and the real-moor pipeline

The game is a single-page web application. There is no game engine and no heavy framework, just Vite (the build tool) and Three.js (a thin WebGL wrapper), with the whole game written as about thirty plain JavaScript modules.

The voxel world

  • Chunked terrain. The world is divided into 16×64×16 columns of blocks, streamed in and out around the player with a per-frame time budget so the framerate stays smooth.
  • Efficient rendering. Only block faces that touch air are turned into geometry ("culled-face meshing"), with soft shadowing in the corners baked into the mesh ("ambient occlusion") rather than computed by lights every frame.
  • Surveyed terrain. Heights come from the Ordnance Survey elevation model: the height of the land at each point is looked up from the real survey and turned into the block column there. The rivers are carved down their real courses and the coast is cut where the data says the land ends. The result is the actual relief of the moors, not a generated stand-in.
  • Detail on top of the real relief. The smaller stuff is still placed by maths: layered value-noise (fractal Brownian motion), 3D noise for caves, and coordinate hashing for the trees, the ore and the scatter. So the surveyed landscape is dressed and populated deterministically without any of the actual geography being invented.

Two ideas that make the rest simple

The world is a pure function of fixed data. That data is the Ordnance Survey heights, the same on every machine, so every client can compute the identical terrain for itself and nobody ever downloads a map. The server only needs to remember the differences players have made, the blocks they have dug or placed. This keeps multiplayer tiny and joins instant. I prove the two sides agree: a check confirms the landscape the server computes and the landscape the browser computes are the same to the block, so no one ever sees a different moor from anyone else.

Time is a pure function of the clock. The day/night cycle, the seasons, and the train timetable are all computed directly from the real-world time (Date.now() in UTC). Every player's client, and the AI characters, agree on what time it is, what season it is, and where the train is, with no message ever sent between them to synchronise. The shared world stays consistent almost for free.

Saves are stored in the browser (IndexedDB): raw chunk bytes plus a little metadata, per account.

The weather is the real weather

Moorstead's sky is tied to the live conditions at Goathland, a real North York Moors village that also appears in the game. A small service pulls the current forecast from a free, keyless weather API every fifteen minutes, maps it onto the game's own weather states (clear, misty, rain, fog) along with fog distance and rainfall, and drives the sky from that. When the real moor is under mist, so is the game, within the hour. If the feed fails it falls back quietly to a procedural weather cycle, so the world never depends on the network to have weather at all.

4. Where and how it is deployed

Moorstead runs across two very different places at once: a global content-delivery network for the game itself, and a single small computer in my house for everything that has to be live and shared.

Game client
Built with Vite, served as static files from Vercel's CDN at www.moorstead.app.
Backend host
A self-hosted mini-server at home (an "EVO X2"), running a Caddy reverse proxy.
Exposure
A Cloudflare tunnel publishes the home server to the internet with no open inbound ports and automatic TLS.
The join
Vercel quietly forwards the game's /brain and /dash paths to the home server, so the public site and the home backend look like one origin to the browser.

The home server runs three small services behind the proxy:

  • The world relay. A WebSocket server (Python / FastAPI) that carries the multiplayer traffic: presence, chat, and the persistent block edits.
  • The village brain. An HTTP service (FastAPI) in front of a local large language model. This is what the villagers think on.
  • A parish dashboard. A private admin panel for me, kept off the public internet.

Deploying is a two-target push: the static client goes to Vercel, and the same build is mirrored to the home server, which keeps the previous build for instant rollback.

5. Playing together: the multiplayer layer

There is one shared moor for everyone (a fixed seed), relayed through the home server over WebSockets. Because the terrain is computed from the seed on every machine, the only things that travel over the network are edits and presence: who is where, who said what, and which blocks have changed. That is why joining is instant and the bandwidth is trivial.

  • Presence and chat. You see other players move, village chat carries about sixty metres, and the day/night clock is shared by everyone.
  • Worlds by invite. The prefix of your invite code decides which room you land in. Each room shares the same terrain but keeps its own block edits, so different groups can have their own moor without seeing each other's building.
  • Per-account progress. Inventory and ventures are saved per account, whichever world you visit.
  • A resilient connection. The client sorts every disconnect into a clear set of causes, reconnects with exponential backoff and jitter, spots half-open ("zombie") sockets, and holds a short grace period so a backgrounded browser tab does not make you flicker in and out for everyone else.

A separate, walled-off world for children

Moorstead has an entirely separate world for children, sealed off from the adult moor. A child is let into it only by a specific kind of invite token (bairn-*). The server checks that token before anyone can enter that room: a child's invite routes into the children's world and nowhere else, and an adult invite cannot reach it. Children do not share a world with adult strangers.

Inside it, the rules are tighter, and built in:

  • Survival mode is locked on and the creative build-and-destroy tools are hidden, so the world cannot be wiped or grief-built.
  • The real landmarks are protected. Built stone at or above ground cannot be broken inside a landmark (the abbey, the kilns, the standing stones, the stations, the moor crosses), though a child can always dig underneath and mine freely.
  • A guided first hour walks a new child through the basics with a ladder of small milestones.
  • Merlin is kid-safe here. The in-world wizard appears in the children's world too, in a hardened persona that governs what he says.

These are real constraints, the same guardrail-and-oversight thinking I bring to AI systems in my professional work, here pointed at the problem of a child loose in an online sandbox.

How many can play at once

Worth a word on scale, because the architecture has an unusual answer.

The server never simulates the world. Terrain, day and night, the seasons, the weather, the train: every client works those out for itself from the seed and the clock. So adding players adds no world-simulation cost at all. The server's only standing job is to pass around three things: where people are, what they type, and which blocks they change. Edits and chat are occasional. The constant cost is position updates.

That makes position the binding constraint. Everyone's position has to reach everyone else in the same room, so the relay's work in one room grows with the square of the number of people in it. Each room is capped at 15 players so the relay stays responsive; when a room fills, the parish clerk automatically assigns newcomers to an overflow shard of the same world (moor-2, bairns-2, and so on), each with its own block edits but the same terrain. Many rooms can run in parallel across the same box.

Rooms are the trick for going wider. Each invite group lives in its own room, walled off from the others, so the load shards. The server's real ceiling is then how many open connections one small machine can hold across all rooms at once, which for an async Python server runs to the hundreds, into the low thousands. So in principle hundreds of people could be playing across many rooms at the same time, on a computer in my house, with no world ever downloaded or simulated on the server.

Nothing in the design ties the world to one server. Because terrain is deterministic and only edits and presence are relayed, rooms could be split across more relays, or bigger ones, with no central world to become a bottleneck. The headroom is a hardware question, not a design one.

6. Living folk: villagers with real agency

This is the part I am proudest of. In most games an NPC is its dialogue: it stands on its spot and talks when you talk to it. The villagers in the real-moor world are AI-driven in what they do, not only in what they say. They have their own working lives and they get on with them whether or not a player is watching. Around a hundred period villagers now live this way across the moor at once, and the system is deployed and running, not a sketch.

A service on the home server holds each villager's logical state, where they are and what they are about: at a town, walking a road, or riding a train on an errand, and it advances that state in real time. A jet carver in Whitby decides the price is good in Pickering, walks to the station, waits for the scheduled service, rides it down the line, and sells. The game draws all of that: you see folk walking the dales and sitting in the carriages, going somewhere for a reason. You can meet them on the road or board the same train, and what they say is seeded with what they are actually doing.

  • They travel for real, and it takes real time. No teleporting. A journey is walk to the station, wait, ride the timetable, walk on at the other end. If you want to catch someone, you plan to be where they will be.
  • They find their own way. People step around buildings, walls and trees and cross rivers, walls and the railway freely (by fords, gates and crossings), so a villager never gets stuck. Animals are the opposite: walls and rivers and the line actually pen them in, which is why a walled field holds its stock and sheep keep to their moor.
  • The whole world is peopled. The towns and the scattered farms are filled with folk fitting the place: fishers and cobble-men on the coast, jet-cutters and coopers in Whitby, colliers and ironstone men at Rosedale, farmers and drovers on the dale farms, platelayers and porters at the rail towns. A core of the characters are hand-written with deep personas and memory; the rest are filled in around them.
  • You find them by looking at the world. The map shows live markers with what each is up to ("Amos, jet to Pickering"), the minimap shows nearby folk, and the station boards list who is catching which train.
  • The AI decides the actions, safely. The model only ever chooses from a fixed set of moves (go here, board that train, work the trade, buy, sell, hand over a parcel, speak, wait), and the server checks each move is legal before it happens, so a villager can never do anything the world does not allow. If the model is slow or returns nonsense, the villager simply waits. The sim never wedges.
  • Graceful offline. If the server is down, the world falls back to a simpler scripted crowd that potters about and speaks from canned lines. It always works, brain or no brain. It just goes quieter.
7. The brain: local AI, no cloud

The villagers think on large language models I run myself, on a small computer in my house, not on a cloud API. No cloud AI is in the conversation loop, there is no per-message cost, and the chat never leaves my hardware.

  • Tiered for a crowd. The villagers run on a small, fast model I host myself on the box (a Gemma model of about 4 billion parameters), so it can serve a whole moor at once. A larger, more capable model (around 27 billion parameters) sits alongside it on the same machine for the moments that deserve it, and the brain is built to send a real, sustained conversation to the bigger model while everyday chatter stays on the fast one. That routing is the piece I am wiring in now.
  • Private memory. Each villager keeps their own memory of the people they have met: what was said, how much they trust you, what you have given them. They greet a regular differently from a stranger.
  • They answer from the real rules. A retrieval layer feeds a villager the game's actual rules when you ask a "how do I..." question, so the answer about mining, farming or the railway is the real answer, not a guess.
  • A hard rule against making things up. If a villager does not know, it is built to say so, in character ("I don't rightly know"), rather than invent an answer. Truthfulness matters to me. Making a model refuse to make things up is exactly the discipline I care about in my day job, and here it is wired in and tested.
  • Inner lives, cheaply. The deterministic routines and small drifting moods are ordinary maths, run every frame. The language model is not on a tick. It is called only when someone actually speaks to a character, or when a villager reaches a real decision point in their day.
8. A world that improves itself overnight

The same machine that runs the villagers by day does something else by night. When the moor is quiet and the server is otherwise idle, it turns judge and teacher on its own work, and the game wakes up a little better each morning.

  • It marks the villagers' honesty. It quizzes them against the real rules and scores how truthful and in-character the answers were, so drift toward making things up is caught and measured rather than guessed at.
  • It learns their voice. From the day's real conversations it tunes how the characters speak, so the dialect and manner stay consistent and improve over time.
  • It tidies their memories. The day's accumulated villager memories are consolidated, so what they keep stays useful and does not sprawl.
  • It checks the world and the economy. Deterministic sweeps run over the world and the markets overnight (terrain integrity, the economy simulation, regression checks), so problems surface in the small hours, not in front of a player.
  • Nothing ships on a hunch. Every change the night produces is gated by the test suite before it can go live. Improvement is measured, and it has to pass, or it does not land.
9. Merlin: the embodiment of my own AI agent

The most unusual character in Moorstead is Merlin, a glowing Yorkshire wizard who walks the moor. Merlin is not a bespoke game NPC. He is the in-world body of a personal AI agent I already had: my own assistant (I call it "Clint") that runs over WhatsApp and handles my briefings, my tasks, and even its own coding. The same agent now wears three faces:

  • A private assistant. Its original job, helping me over WhatsApp.
  • A remote warden. It runs the live game for me by message: who is online, broadcasting notices, moving players between worlds.
  • Merlin, an in-world character. The one other players meet inside the game.

Merlin is a walled-off persona. The in-world wizard has its own empty memory and only game tools, with no path to my personal data. The separation is deliberate. It is a security boundary, so that nobody can use in-game chat to coax private information out of the assistant that runs my life.

What Merlin can actually do

  • He knows where you are. Merlin reads live game state (your position, your inventory, the geography of the world) and gives exact, grounded directions ("south-west, about twelve hundred blocks, to the Rosedale kilns").
  • He comes when called. Say his name anywhere on the moor and he is conjured to your side within seconds. Several players can each summon their own Merlin at once.
  • He holds a conversation. Once he is with you, you can just keep talking. He follows you and answers without needing his name each time.
  • He leads and he builds. Ask him to take you to iron or to a landmark and he physically walks you there. Ask for help and he casts "spells" that place real blocks in the world (a ring of lanterns, a foundation, a standing stone) through the multiplayer relay.

The agent can also write the game

Because Merlin is a real agent, I can ask it in plain English, over WhatsApp, to add new content to the game itself. It drives a coding model to write a small, additive feature (a new animal, say), runs that change through a mechanical safety gate that blocks anything touching the engine, world generation, or security, then through the full test suite and a build, and finally proposes it (or, when I explicitly arm it, deploys it). A grazing llama and a robin in Moorstead were written this way. Risky or far-reaching changes are refused by the gate by design, and I promote anything that reaches the public site by hand.

10. What is genuinely new here, and what is not

I'd rather be precise than promotional. None of the individual ingredients is a world first. Voxel sandboxes are a mature genre. Building a game world from real terrain data is done (flight simulators, some map-based games). Self-coding AI agents exist (the "Voyager" Minecraft agent). LLM-driven NPC middleware exists (Inworld, Convai, NVIDIA's ACE). Generative-agent "memory and routine" simulations exist (Stanford's "Smallville" study). Models that score and fine-tune other models are now standard practice. Chat-operated admin bots are common. Procedural, asset-free graphics are an old demoscene tradition.

A few things here are not routine, and one or two I think are genuinely new.

1. What Fable 5 could do, and why it matters beyond a game

I have built a fair number of games with AI by now, and there is a failure mode I had come to expect. The model produces all the elements (a map, enemies, items, rooms) but with no real grasp of how they will interact, or how a person will actually experience them. An enemy gets placed where the player will never meet it. A door opens onto nothing. The parts are all present, and the lived experience is incoherent. The model has assembled a set, not a world.

Fable 5 was the first model I used that did not do this. It could reason through the experience itself: what the world needed to feel like from inside it, how a player would move through it, where the moments would land. To my eye that crossed into something near the threshold people mean by AGI. It was not encyclopaedic recall. It was an apparent grasp of lived, in-world experience, a working analogue of how a person actually inhabits a situation, which is itself one of the stated markers of general intelligence. It did not feel like autocomplete. It felt like working with something that understood what it was making, almost, at moments, a kind of awareness. That is why the architecture in Section 3 fell into place so quickly: deriving the world, the clock, and the weather from shared truths, so the whole thing holds together with almost no synchronised state. Getting that right is not about writing more code. It is about understanding what the system has to be. When Fable 5 was banned, the project carried on with a weaker model, because by then the part that needed real reasoning was already done.

This is the lesson I take back to my day job. I'm a commercial litigator, and I build AI systems for legal work. The flow of professional software, how a document or a workflow or a chain of legal reasoning actually moves and connects for the person using it, is every bit as tricky as the flow of a game. The difference is that a game is honest. If the experience is broken you can see it: the enemy stands in the corner doing nothing. In legal or financial software the same incoherence is invisible until it fails in front of a client or a court. A game is one of the few places you can watch a model reason about lived experience and tell at once whether it got it right. I have come to think that building games is a badly undervalued training ground for anyone making serious software in law, finance, or any field where the flow matters and is hard to check.

2. An agent that inhabits, operates, and writes its own world

The part I think is genuinely new is structural. In almost every system, three things are kept firmly apart: the content of a product (its characters, its world), the operation of it (the live service and its admins), and the development of it (the people and pipelines that ship new code). In Moorstead, a single agent is all three at once. As Merlin, it is a character you can walk up to and talk to, content standing inside the world. As the warden, it runs the live service: presence, broadcasts, moving players between worlds. As the auto-coder, it writes and ships new content into the running game on request. The world is therefore, in part, grown from inside itself, by one of its own inhabitants.

Each of those facets has prior art on its own: self-coding agents, NPC middleware, chat-ops bots. The union is the part I cannot find anywhere else, one agent that is at the same time an inhabitant, the operator, and the author of the same live service. Two deliberate boundaries keep it sane rather than reckless. A security membrane walls the public, in-world Merlin off from the private assistant that runs my life, so in-game chat can never be used to coax out personal data. A mechanical gate refuses to let the auto-coder touch the engine, world generation, authentication, or anything load-bearing, confining it to small additive content that must still pass the full test suite and my own promotion before it can reach the public site. The villagers, and Merlin himself, think on my hardware at zero marginal cost, which is the only reason a world full of live AI characters is affordable to run at all.

3. A populated world that runs, and improves, on one box at home

The other thing I have not seen put together is the whole of the real-moor world running on a single computer in a house. Each ingredient exists somewhere. The combination is the part I cannot find: a real place rebuilt from Ordnance Survey data, filled with villagers who are AI-driven in their actions and not just their speech, all thinking on local models with no cloud in the loop, tiered so one box can serve a crowd, held to a hard rule against making things up, and then improving themselves overnight on the same machine, with every change gated by tests before it lands. None of that depends on a paid API or anyone else's servers. It is the kind of thing people assume needs a data centre, and it sits behind a tunnel in my house.

The honesty rule deserves a line of its own, because it is the same thing I care about professionally. A villager that says "I don't rightly know" instead of inventing an answer is a small, domestic version of the problem I work on in legal AI: stopping a model asserting what it cannot support. Here I can watch it work, and the overnight routine measures whether it is still working. That feedback loop, a model marking its own truthfulness against the real rules and being held to the result, is the bit I would most want to carry across to serious software.

And my own discipline is built into the safety design

I'm a commercial litigator. My working life is adversarial scrutiny: guardrails, auditability, and never trusting an assertion I cannot verify. It shows here, in the engineering rather than as a slogan. The NPC prompts are hardened against drifting off-script, and retried when they do. Merlin is walled off against exfiltration. The auto-coder is fenced by a gate that assumes it will eventually try something it should not. For "visual" correctness, the test is whether you can actually see out of the train cab, and I prove it by casting rays through the scene rather than trusting a screenshot. When your implementer is a model you must not trust blindly, the expertise that matters most is the expertise in not trusting blindly. That, more than any single feature, is what I brought to a codebase I never typed.

The honest summary is a few claims and a caveat. The new things are a live world inhabited, operated, and extended by one agent that is also one of its own characters, and a real place rebuilt from Ordnance Survey data and peopled by villagers who act, not just talk, who think on local models, refuse to make things up, and improve themselves overnight, all on one box at home. The wider lesson, the one I care about most, is that the rare ingredient was not code. It was a model that could reason about lived experience, and the same discipline of holding a model to the truth that I bring to legal work. Those are exactly the capabilities serious software needs and rarely gets to test honestly, and a game is the best honest test of them I know. The caveat is that this is one project by one person. I offer it as evidence, not as a theorem.

Moorstead is a browser game: you open a link, nothing installs. This page explains, in plain English and in technical terms, what the game can and cannot do to your machine, what data I see, how the worlds are walled off, and what happens if someone misbehaves.

Your machine: what Moorstead can and cannot do

In plain English

  • No download. There is no installer, no .exe, no app store package, and no browser extension. You play in a normal tab.
  • No access to your files. The game cannot read your documents, photos, or other folders. It uses only what browsers allow games to use: memory, graphics, and optional local storage inside the browser.
  • No ads or third-party trackers. There are no advertising networks, analytics pixels, or social-media widgets. The client sends a once-per-session landing ping to my server (browser id only) so I can see who visited; see below.
  • No cryptocurrency or background mining. Nothing in the client hashes coins or runs hidden work when the tab is closed.
  • Network use is limited. If you play a solo single-player world, after the first load it can run from your browser alone. Multiplayer, villager chat, and login talk to my home server. Weather optionally reads a public forecast for Goathland.

Technically (for the curious)

Delivery
Static files built with Vite, served over HTTPS from Vercel's CDN at www.moorstead.app. Subresource integrity is enforced by same-origin loading; a Content-Security-Policy header restricts scripts to this origin only.
Runtime
WebGL via Three.js inside the browser sandbox. No eval, no native bindings, no WebAssembly modules from third parties.
Dependencies
One production npm package: three. Vite is dev-only and never ships to players.
Local storage
IndexedDB holds solo-world chunk saves. localStorage holds a per-browser id, your invite login (if you use one), and display preferences. Nothing leaves the browser unless you join the shared moor or talk to villagers.
Third parties
Open-Meteo (optional weather, keyless). Everything else multiplayer-related goes to moorstead.sovren.xyz (my Cloudflare-tunnelled home server), proxied through the same site origin for /brain and /dash.

What I collect (and why)

If you only play solo worlds, I see nothing. If you use the shared moor or villager chat, I necessarily see some session data to run the service and keep order:

  • Site landing. Once per browser session when you open the game: a browser device id and optional display name (if logged in). No page URLs or referrer tracking.
  • Invite requests. If you ask for an adult invite, your email address and optional name/note are stored on my server until I approve or reject the request. Children's invites are not handled through the public form.
  • Login / invite claim. Your invite code (validated once), chosen display name, and a browser device id, to bind your account and world room.
  • Session heartbeat. About once a minute while playing: coarse progress (day count, standing, location name), display name, and your IP address as seen by the server.
  • Multiplayer chat. In-world speech within ~60 metres is logged on the server with timestamp, name, position, and text (profanity filtered). This is the audit trail for moderation.
  • Villager conversations. Processed on my local large language model at home, not sent to OpenAI or similar. Named villagers keep per-player memory on that server.
  • Crash reports. If the client throws an error, a truncated message and stack trace may be sent over the multiplayer connection (throttled, capped at 600 characters).
  • World saves (shared moor). Inventory and quest progress for invited accounts are stored on my server so they follow you between visits.

I do not sell data. I do not run advertising. Retention is practical, not legalistic: logs exist to debug, moderate, and restore worlds.

How worlds are walled off

Each invite group plays in its own room (shared terrain seed, separate block edits). When you log in with an invite code, the parish ledger issues a short-lived session token tied to your account id and your room. The multiplayer relay refuses connections that do not match: you cannot enter the children's room without a bairn-* invite, and you cannot use a child's token on the adult moor. Ramblers without an invite may walk the open moor room only.

Villager AI runs on my hardware. Merlin (the wizard) is a walled-off persona with no path to my private assistant data. In-game chat cannot be used to extract personal information from the system that runs my life outside the game.

Expected behaviour, and what happens if you break it

Moorstead is a shared home, not a free-for-all. I run it for friends and family on a small machine in my house. Everyone is welcome who treats the place and the people in it with respect. Abuse is not tolerated.

What counts as a breach

  • Griefing. Deliberately wrecking builds, spamming block edits to lag or ruin the world, harassing players, or trying to enter a room you were not invited to (especially the children's world).
  • Harassment or hate. Slurs, threats, sexual content, or sustained targeting of another player in chat (in-world or villager prompts used to abuse others).
  • Impersonation or evasion. Pretending to be the warden, using alt accounts to dodge a ban, or sharing invite codes publicly when they were meant for one household.
  • Attacking the service. Flooding the relay or brain, probing admin endpoints, or attempting to bypass session or room checks.
  • Prompt abuse. Trying to jailbreak villagers or Merlin into off-script, harmful, or exfiltration attempts (these are logged and reviewed).

What I do about it

  • Immediate disconnect. Banned player ids and display names are checked on every multiplayer connection. A match is refused at the door with a plain message.
  • Chat log. In-world speech is appended to a server log with time, name, and position, enough to decide what happened without ambiguity.
  • Fast exclusion. For clear griefing or harassment I add the offender to the ban list on the server. There is no appeal bureaucracy: this is a private game on private hardware. Serious or repeated cases lose their invite permanently.
  • Children's world. The bairns room is the strictest. Any adult stranger who reaches it without a valid child invite, or any player who misbehaves there, is removed and banned without a second warning.
  • Operator response. I am the parish warden. Reports reach me through the session logs and, for people I know, directly. I aim to act within hours for abuse, not days.

If you see something wrong, note the player's display name and roughly where/when it happened, and tell me. I would rather ban one fool than let a whole room suffer.

Your invite code is a key

Treat your invite like a password. Anyone with the code can claim the account name and enter your world room. Do not post codes on public forums. If a code leaks, tell me and I will mint a fresh one and retire the old.

Scale and room sharding

Each multiplayer room holds at most 15 people at once. When you log in, if your usual room is full, you are quietly placed in the next shard of the same world family (dale-2, bairns-3, etc.). Same moor, separate building, like parallel copies of the same map. I can add more shards in seconds; no new invite codes are needed.

Honest limits

  • This is a hobby project on a home server, not a bank or a Fortune 500 SaaS. I apply the same guardrail thinking I use in my professional work, but capacity and uptime are those of one box behind a tunnel.
  • No system is perfectly secure. I reduce risk by keeping the client tiny, the attack surface small, sessions token-bound, and admin tools off the public internet. You further reduce risk by using an up-to-date browser and not sharing invites.
  • If you need a guarantee no one will ever see your IP or chat, play a solo single-player world only. That mode sends nothing to my server.
